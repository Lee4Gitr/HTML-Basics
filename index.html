There are many other types of tags as well. Here is an "ol" tag, or Ordered List.

Here is a "p" tag, or paragraph tag. While it will render very similarly, it's important to understand their differences. One very important rule is: You cannot nest a div inside of a "p" tag.

A nice element to use to break between import paragraphs is a horizontal rule, or "hr" element. It adds some nice spacing between other elements.

Block vs. Inline

Now the important part... These have all been block level elements. This means they take up room on a web page. There are different types of elements that DON'T take up space, and that was just one of them. These are called "inline" elements. They include the b or Bold tag, the i or italic tag, the span tag, and the two most important inline tags, the image tag , which must have a "src" or source attribute specifying where the image is, and an "alt" text attribute, which will display if the image isn't loaded or if someone is using a screen reader. Alt attributes aren't strictly necessary, but they are a "best practice" when talking about modern websites. They make it easier for people of all abilities to use your site. As you can see, the image tag doesn't create a new area, it just renders along with the other text! R-i-d-i-c-u-l-o-u-s.

The final inline element you need to know is the "a" or anchor tag. This can be used to link to link to other sites, or used to navigate to another page on your website! As a matter of fact, lets do that! Click me!
